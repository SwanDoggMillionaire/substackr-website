Most Substack writers spend all their time looking forward — the next post, the next subscriber milestone, the next idea. Very few ever stop and look at the whole picture from the outside.

That is a mistake. Because the way your newsletter looks to a new reader — its niche clarity, its positioning, its About page — is often very different from the picture you have in your head.

A proper audit fixes that. This guide covers how to do one, both manually and with the free tool we built for exactly this purpose.

## What a Substack audit actually covers

A newsletter audit is not about metrics. Open rates and subscriber counts are lagging indicators — they tell you what already happened. An audit is about the structural factors that drive those numbers: how clearly you've defined what you write about, how well your positioning holds up against similar writers, and whether your About page does the job of converting a curious first-time visitor into a subscriber.

A good audit examines six things:

1. **Niche and topic focus** — Are you writing about one clear thing, or is it a mix of whatever you felt like that week? Clarity here is the single biggest predictor of subscriber growth.
2. **Audience resonance** — Who is actually reading this? Are you writing for the audience you have or the one you want?
3. **Positioning** — How are you different from the fifty other newsletters covering a similar area? What is your distinct angle?
4. **Content patterns** — Are there formats and topics that consistently perform? Are there gaps you keep avoiding?
5. **Monetisation alignment** — If you have paid subscriptions (or plan to), does your content strategy support that?
6. **About page** — This is the highest-leverage page on your Substack. Most writers write it once, forget about it, and leave money on the table.

## Method 2: Do it manually (the slow version)

If you want to understand the thinking behind the automated audit, or if you want to go deeper on a specific area, here is how to do it yourself.

### 1. Niche clarity check

Open your last twelve posts. Read just the titles. Now answer this question: if someone landed on your Substack having never heard of you, what would they think your newsletter is about?

If the answer is obvious — you write about building a solo consulting business, or about the science of endurance sport, or about how small businesses use AI — you have good niche clarity.

If the answer is "it depends" or "a bit of everything", you have a problem. Readers do not subscribe to a writer who covers everything. They subscribe to a writer who covers one thing with unusual depth and an interesting angle.

The fix is not to delete old posts. It is to decide, going forward, what you are really about — and write the next ten posts from that position. Niche drift is reversible.

### 2. About page review

Your About page has one job: convert a curious first-time reader into a subscriber. Most Substack About pages fail at this because they were written as an introduction to the writer rather than a promise to the reader.

Check yours against these four questions:

- **Within five seconds, can a stranger understand exactly what your newsletter covers?** If your first sentence is about you rather than about them, it is probably not landing.
- **Is there a specific promise?** "I write about business" is not a promise. "Every Tuesday I break down one strategy that helped a company go from zero to €10m" is a promise.
- **Is there social proof?** A subscriber count, a notable quote, a publication mention — anything that signals other people have found this worth reading.
- **Is the subscribe button visible without scrolling?** Substack puts one below the fold by default. If yours is buried, move your content around so the CTA appears sooner.

### 3. Content pattern analysis

Export or scroll through your post archive. Group posts by topic and format. After twenty or more posts, patterns emerge — and so do gaps.

Common things to look for:

- **The format you avoid** — If you never write list posts, or never write personal essays, ask whether the avoidance is intentional or just habit
- **The topic you circle** — Most writers have one topic they return to constantly, sometimes without realising. Is that the topic your audience responds to most? If so, you may be underselling it in your niche definition.
- **The post that overperformed** — Most writers have one post that got unexpectedly shared or grew the list meaningfully. What was different about it? Can you reverse-engineer the formula?

### 4. The positioning gap

Search Substack for three or four newsletters covering similar ground to yours. Read their About pages and recent posts. Now ask: what is the thing they are not covering? What perspective is missing from this space?

That gap is your opportunity. The writers who grow fastest in competitive niches are rarely the best writers — they are the ones who found and owned a specific angle that nobody else had claimed.

## What to do after your audit

An audit is only useful if it results in a decision. The worst outcome is reading a detailed analysis of your newsletter and then carrying on exactly as before because changing feels uncomfortable.

The most common and highest-leverage actions after an audit:

1. **Rewrite your About page** — this takes an afternoon and pays off indefinitely
2. **Narrow your stated niche** — even one sentence in your header that sharpens what you are about will help
3. **Write the post you have been avoiding** — most audits surface one conspicuous gap. That gap usually represents something you know you should write about but have been putting off
4. **Post your audit findings publicly** — writing a post called "here is what I discovered when I audited my own newsletter" is almost always interesting to your audience and good for building trust

## Frequently asked questions

**How accurate is the automated audit?**

The accuracy depends on how much content you have published. With twelve or more posts and a complete About page, the analysis tends to be specific and useful. With fewer posts or a sparse About page, it is more general. The tool tells you the confidence level on every result.

**Does it need access to my Substack account?**

No. The tool uses Substack's public API endpoints — the same data that anyone can see when they visit your newsletter. You do not need to log in or share any credentials.

**How often should I audit my newsletter?**

Once when you are starting out to establish your baseline. Then again after every significant change — a new niche direction, a redesign, a shift in posting frequency. After that, once or twice a year is plenty. The strategic picture does not change that quickly.

**My newsletter is new — is it worth auditing?**

Yes, but for a different reason. With fewer than ten posts, the audit will be less accurate about your patterns. But the About page analysis is always useful, and the niche clarity check is especially valuable early on, before you have spent years writing in the wrong direction.
