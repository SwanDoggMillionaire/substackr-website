The advice to "pick a niche" is universally given and almost universally misapplied.

"Finance." "Productivity." "Health and wellness." These are categories, not niches. They describe a shelf in a bookshop, not a publication with a distinct reader and a clear point of view. Every writer who picks a category-level topic starts with an invisible ceiling: there's no way to be the obvious choice for anyone.

The specificity problem is why most newsletters stay small. Not bad writing. Not inconsistent publishing. The niche is too broad to mean anything.

## What a niche actually is

A niche is the overlap of three things: a defined reader, a specific problem that reader has, and a lens only you can bring.

"Personal finance" covers millions of people with thousands of different problems. "Personal finance for freelancers navigating self-employment tax for the first time" covers a specific person with a specific problem at a specific moment. The second is a niche. The first is a category.

A practical test: if you can name five newsletters you'd be directly competing with, you're at category level. If you struggle to name two, you're in niche territory.

Lenny Rachitsky's newsletter succeeded not because he wrote about "startups" but because he focused on product management at growth-stage B2B companies, drawing on his years running growth at Airbnb. That combination of audience (PMs), context (growth-stage), and credential (Airbnb) created something that felt specifically useful to a specific person. He wasn't the startup newsletter. He was the newsletter for PMs trying to figure out how the best companies actually build product.

## Why categories feel safe

The category trap happens because broad topics feel like broad opportunity. If you write about "marketing," you can reach marketers, founders, freelancers, students. The logic seems sound.

The reality is that targeting everyone means resonating with no one. A marketer at a Fortune 500 consumer brand and a freelancer doing social media for local businesses both technically work in marketing. They have almost nothing in common. A newsletter that tries to serve both serves neither well.

Packy McCormick's Not Boring covers business, which sounds category-level. But Not Boring isn't really a business newsletter. It's a newsletter with a specific thesis (that ambitious companies are net positive for the world) delivered through long-form essays. Readers don't subscribe to learn about business. They subscribe for Packy's take on business. That's a defensible position. "Business news" isn't.

The distinction matters: a *perspective* on a category is a niche. A category itself is not.

## The anatomy of a working niche

Strong niches tend to follow a pattern: **[reader identity] + [specific problem or transformation] + [distinctive lens].**

Chenell Basilio's Growth in Reverse covers newsletter growth, but the format makes it specific: forensic reverse-engineering of how the best newsletters actually grew. Not general tips. A method applied consistently to a single question for a well-defined reader.

Ben Thompson's Stratechery covers technology business for professionals who want strategic frameworks rather than news recaps. The lens (business strategy drawn from economic history) is specific enough that readers know exactly what they're subscribing to.

Anne-Laure Le Cunff's Ness Labs sits at the intersection of neuroscience and productivity, making academic research accessible for knowledge workers. The combination of audience and subject is narrow enough to own.

None of these are category-level. Each answers clearly: who is this for, and what does it do for them?

If you can't answer that in one sentence, the niche isn't specific enough yet.

## Finding your angle

**Start with your experiential edge.** The strongest niches come from something you know at a level most people don't, built from specific experience rather than passing interest. Lenny had years running growth at Airbnb. Chenell had studied hundreds of newsletter growth stories before anyone else thought to systematise that knowledge.

Ask: where have I accumulated specific, non-obvious knowledge that most people in a given field don't have?

**Use the intersection test.** Strong niches often sit at the junction of two things most people don't combine. "Productivity for night-shift nurses." "B2B content strategy for companies operating under non-disclosure constraints." "Personal finance for professional athletes in their 20s." Each combines a specific reader with a specific constraint. The intersection is unusual and useful.

Write down three audiences you could credibly write for, and three specific problems within each. The overlaps that feel almost too narrow are usually worth investigating.

**The 20-issue test.** Before committing to a niche, ask: can you write 20 issues without repeating yourself? Running dry at eight means the niche is too narrow to sustain. Seeing 50 issues clearly might mean you're still too broad, or you've found something genuinely rich. ## Common too-broad niches and how to fix them

**"Personal development"**: for whom, at what stage, around what obstacle? "Personal development for engineers moving into management for the first time" is a niche. "Personal development" is a self-help section.

**"Marketing"**: specify industry, company stage, or channel. "B2B demand generation for SaaS companies under £10M ARR" means something. "Marketing" doesn't.

**"Mental health"**: fix by audience and context. "Mental health for founders navigating company failure" has a recognisable reader. "Mental health" competes with hospitals, charities, and Instagram.

**"AI and tech"**: the most crowded category in publishing right now. Fix by choosing one industry vertical (AI for healthcare operations, AI for independent game developers) or one specific role (how CTOs at mid-market companies are actually thinking about AI adoption in 2026). General AI update newsletters are running on borrowed time as the field matures and specialist voices emerge.

The test: if a generalist media company could publish your newsletter without it feeling out of place, the niche needs work.

## Why narrow niches grow faster

The counterintuitive truth is that specific newsletters grow faster from zero. A smaller, well-defined audience is easier to reach and far more likely to refer others.

A newsletter for "remote workers" has an addressable audience in the tens of millions and no natural community. A newsletter for "remote-first engineering managers navigating distributed team culture" has a smaller audience, but those people talk to each other, share the same Slack communities, and will recommend the newsletter to colleagues who match exactly. Word of mouth scales when readers can instantly picture someone else who'd want it.

Starting narrow also closes off the exits. A broad niche gives you infinite justifications to pivot, add topics, or hedge. A specific niche forces the question: do I know this subject well enough to own it? Writers who answer yes tend to produce better work and build stronger reader relationships.

Trying to be broad from the start is how you end up with a newsletter nobody can describe in a sentence, including you.
